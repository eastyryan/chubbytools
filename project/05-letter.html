A letter from the workshop —

About a small thing, made carefully.

A carpenter pencil shouldn't be the kind of object you think about. Ours isn't, most of the time. It sits in your apron, gets sharpened with a knife, levels a brick, marks a stud, snaps onto a piece of scaffold so it doesn't get lost in the dust.

But ours is also entirely made of plastic that already had a life. Bottles, tubs, lids — collected, cleaned, and re-formed into the only carpenter pencil we know of that never cost a tree.

If you've used a Chubby's, you already know. If you haven't, perhaps this letter will do.

Warmly, — The Chubby's Workshop

The Virtues

Four small things, each one earned.

We didn't design a pencil to win awards. We designed one a mason would reach for first thing on a Monday. These are the four reasons they do.

I.

It cost no trees.

The body is moulded from 100% recycled plastic. There is no virgin material, and there is, for once, no felled timber.

II.

It doesn't get lost.

An integrated magnet inside the body snaps the pencil to steel scaffold, joists, or the side of a toolbox. A small thing that saves an hour a week.

III.

Real graphite, not pressed lead.

Marks cleaner on rough timber, sharpens with a knife instead of crumbling, and lasts about three times as long as the ordinary stuff.

IV.

It fits where torpedo levels won't.

Originally drawn up for masons working tight to soffits. Carpenters and tile-setters found it shortly thereafter.

"No trees were hurt in the making of this pencil."
— Our small promise
On its use

Made for masons. Adopted by everyone.

We drew the first one for stonemasons. Then a tiler used one to mark ceramic. Then a finish carpenter levelled stringers. Then an electrician installed a light box with one in his teeth, and now we just stop expecting to be surprised.

It turns out the smallest tool on the site is also the most universal.
